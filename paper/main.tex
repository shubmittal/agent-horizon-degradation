\begin{abstract}
Agent benchmarks report steadily rising success rates, yet deployed LLM agents
remain unreliable on long, multi-step tasks. We argue much of this tension is an
artifact of \emph{horizon}: benchmarks score agents at task lengths where success is
still high, while production demands far longer horizons. We measure the effect
directly. Across 10{,}664 controlled agent trajectories spanning nine models
(1.2B--671B parameters and four proprietary deployed models, across six vendor
families), four task families---including a genuinely agentic tool-use loop---five
horizons ($H\!\in\!\{2,4,8,16,32\}$ dependent steps), and three context regimes, we
characterize the \emph{shape} of degradation and disentangle its cause. Task success
follows a geometric law, $P(\text{success})\approx a\,\rstep^{\horizon}$, where
$\rstep$ is a model- and task-specific per-step reliability: geometric is the
best-fitting shape in $28/36$ model$\times$task cells. Per-step reliability rises with
model scale (Pearson $r{=}0.36$ vs.\ $\log$ parameters) and saturates near $0.7$ even
for the strongest models and deployed systems---so geometric compounding guarantees
collapse at long horizons for every model we tested. The effect is sharpest on the
\emph{agentic} task: on a multi-hop tool-use loop, every model---including GPT-4o-mini,
Gemini, and Claude---falls from $\sim$1.0 success at $H{=}2$ to near zero by $H{=}16$.
Degradation also \emph{accelerates} within a trajectory (per-step accuracy $0.58\to
0.44$ from the first to the last third). Disentangling step-count from context-length,
the rot is intrinsic to executing many dependent steps, not raw context length:
bounding the context window \emph{steepens} decay ($p{=}3\times10^{-6}$), the opposite
of a lost-in-the-middle prediction, so naive context truncation backfires. Projecting
measured reliability onto benchmark horizons shows the gap: mean projected success
falls from $0.42$ at GAIA-length tasks to $0.30$ at SWE-bench-length to $0.24$ at
100-step production horizons. We release all code, prompts, seeds, and raw
trajectories.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Large language model (LLM) agents---models that perceive context, plan, call tools,
and update state in a loop---now score highly on a fast-growing suite of benchmarks
\citep{jimenez2024swebench,yao2024taubench,zhou2023webarena,mialon2023gaia}, and the
time horizon of tasks they can complete has been doubling rapidly
\citep{kwa2025metr}. Yet practitioners who deploy agents report a stubborn gap: agents
that pass benchmark tasks remain unreliable on the long, open-ended workflows
production demands.

We take a specific position on why. A task has a \emph{horizon}: the number of
dependent steps an agent must execute correctly. If each step succeeds independently
with probability $\rstep<1$, a task of horizon $\horizon$ succeeds with probability
$\approx \rstep^{\horizon}$---close to $\rstep$ for short tasks but collapsing
geometrically as $\horizon$ grows. Benchmarks predominantly sample short-to-medium
horizons; production agents operate an order of magnitude longer. If degradation is
geometric, then \emph{benchmarks sample the flat top of the decay curve}, and the
production gap is an expected consequence of horizon rather than noise.

We test this empirically, characterizing the \emph{law} (the functional form of
success vs.\ horizon) and \emph{disentangling its cause} (step count vs.\ context
length). Contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{A measured law of horizon decay.} On controlled, oracle-verifiable agent
  tasks---including a genuinely agentic tool-use loop---success follows a geometric law
  $P\approx a\,\rstep^{\horizon}$ (best fit in $28/36$ model$\times$task cells;
  \S\ref{sec:law}). The single quantity $\rstep$ governs it.
\item \textbf{The effect holds for deployed models and real agent loops.} Nine models
  including GPT-4o-mini, Gemini, and Claude all decay; on the agentic task they collapse
  from $\sim$1.0 to near zero by $H{=}16$ (\S\ref{sec:agentic}).
\item \textbf{Degradation accelerates} within a trajectory ($0.58\to0.44$ per-step
  accuracy), a super-geometric tail (\S\ref{sec:accel}).
\item \textbf{Cause: step count, not context length.} Bounding context \emph{steepens}
  decay; the rot is intrinsic to many dependent steps, and naive context truncation
  backfires (\S\ref{sec:cause}).
\item \textbf{The benchmark gap, quantified} (\S\ref{sec:gap}).
\end{enumerate}
The study is deliberately reproducible: synthetic tasks with exact oracles (no
LLM-judge noise), nine public/deployed models, recorded seeds, and a released dataset.
Our novelty over prior reports that agents degrade with length \citep{kwa2025metr,
laban2025lostconv} is the \emph{controlled disentangling} of step-count from
context-length and the \emph{geometric-law} characterization with a directly measured
per-step reliability.

\section{Related Work}
\textbf{Agent benchmarks} evaluate agents on software issues
\citep{jimenez2024swebench}, tool-and-user interaction
\citep{yao2024taubench,barres2025tau2bench}, web navigation
\citep{zhou2023webarena,deng2023mind2web}, general assistance \citep{mialon2023gaia},
OS/app control \citep{xie2024osworld,trivedi2024appworld}, function calling
\citep{patil2025bfcl}, multi-environment suites \citep{liu2024agentbench}, and company
workflows \citep{xu2024agentcompany}. They report aggregate single-attempt success and
span short to very long horizons, but rarely a horizon-resolved decay curve.
\citet{kwa2025metr} track the \emph{time horizon} of completable tasks across model
generations; we fix the models and resolve success \emph{against} horizon in a
controlled environment.

\textbf{Evaluation validity and reliability.} \citet{kapoor2024agents} document
cost-blindness and evaluation shortcomings; \citet{zhu2025abc} establish best practices
for rigorous agentic benchmarks; both motivate horizon-aware, reliability-oriented
measurement. \citet{cemri2025mast} taxonomize multi-agent failures; our compounding
result quantifies their core observation that local errors cascade.

\textbf{Long context and compounding.} A complementary line studies degradation with
input position \citep{liu2024lostmiddle}, with input length at fixed task
\citep{levy2024moretokens}, and on synthetic long-context probes
\citep{hsieh2024ruler,modarressi2025nolima}. \citet{dziri2023faithfate} show
transformer accuracy decays rapidly with compositional complexity, and
\citet{laban2025lostconv} show large single- to multi-turn drops. These predict
\emph{context length} drives agent degradation; our disentangling tests this directly
and finds the opposite---bounding context worsens performance, implicating step count.

\textbf{Agent methods.} ReAct-style reasoning-and-acting \citep{yao2023react} and
verbal self-reflection \citep{shinn2023reflexion} are the dominant loops; our agentic
family uses a ReAct loop. We study unmodified loops to measure intrinsic degradation.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}
\subsection{Task families}
We use four synthetic families, each a tool-using agent loop with a ground-truth
simulator that verifies success \emph{exactly} (no LLM judge). Three are
\emph{streaming} state-tracking tasks with $O(1)$--$O(k)$ per-step state, so step count
and context length can be varied independently (\S\ref{sec:regimes}); the fourth is a
\emph{genuinely agentic} tool-use loop. Each family stresses a distinct capability:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{ledger} (arithmetic working memory): maintain three account balances
  through $\horizon$ operations; report a queried balance.
\item \textbf{refchain} (long-range retrieval): track one announced variable through
  $\horizon$ assignments amid distractors; report its final value.
\item \textbf{cipher} (procedural execution): apply $\horizon$ ordered string edits to
  a 5-character string; report the result.
\item \textbf{toolqa} (agentic tool use): the agent must reach a node $\horizon$ hops
  along a hidden chain by issuing its own \texttt{inspect} tool calls---it cannot
  pre-plan, since each hop's target is revealed only by inspecting the previous node---
  then report the destination's key. This is a real ReAct loop: the agent chooses its
  actions.
\end{itemize}
In the streaming families, each step delivers one instruction and the agent emits a
JSON state; no correctness feedback is given, so per-step errors compound and are
measured. The simulator scores each state and the final answer against the oracle.

\subsection{Context regimes: disentangling step count from context length}\label{sec:regimes}
For the streaming families, at a fixed horizon the number of operations is held equal
across three regimes that vary only the two axes of interest: \textbf{natural}
($\horizon$ turns, full history), \textbf{compressed} ($\horizon$ turns, short context
via carried state and windowed history), and \textbf{padded} (1 turn, all $\horizon$
operations in one prompt). natural-vs-compressed isolates context length;
natural-vs-padded isolates the multi-step process. The same instance is reused across
regimes (paired). The agentic family runs only the natural regime.

\subsection{Models, design, and metrics}
We evaluate nine instruction-tuned models via OpenRouter: six open
(Llama-3.2-1B, Qwen2.5-7B, Llama-3.1-8B, Llama-3.3-70B, Qwen2.5-72B, DeepSeek-V3;
1.2B--671B, known sizes) and three proprietary deployed models (GPT-4o-mini, Gemini
2.5 Flash-Lite, Claude-3-Haiku; sizes undisclosed). The streaming design is
$9\times3\times5\times3\times25$ trajectories; the agentic family adds
$9\times4\times15$ (natural). Total analyzed: 10{,}664. Temperature is fixed at $0.2$;
seeds are recorded. We report per-cell success with Wilson 95\% intervals; fit
geometric/threshold/linear shapes and select by AIC; estimate per-step reliability
$\rstep$ directly as the fraction of individual steps correct; and fit
$\mathrm{logit}\,P=\beta_0+\beta_H\log_2\!\horizon\cdot C(\text{regime})$ for the
disentangling. Hypotheses, design, and analysis plan were pre-registered before the
full run; deviations are logged in the released repository.

\section{Results}
\subsection{Degradation follows a geometric law}\label{sec:law}
Figure~\ref{fig:decay} shows success vs.\ horizon for every model and family. Success
declines monotonically with horizon wherever the task is non-trivial for the model.
Selecting among geometric, threshold, and linear forms by AIC, the \textbf{geometric
form wins in $28$ of $36$} model$\times$task cells; the rest are threshold-shaped---the
``cliff'' where strong models hold near-perfect accuracy until $\horizon{=}32$. The
geometric law is thus the dominant description, and per-step reliability $\rstep$ is the
quantity that governs it.

The magnitude of decay is governed by $\rstep$, which varies with model and task
difficulty (Table~\ref{tab:rstep}). On \texttt{refchain} the strong models have
$\rstep\!\approx\!1$ and show essentially no decay (a ceiling); on \texttt{cipher} even
the best models have low $\rstep$ and collapse within a few steps. The same models thus
appear robust or fragile depending entirely on per-step reliability, exactly as
$P\approx \rstep^{\horizon}$ predicts. Whether degradation is \emph{visible} depends on
$\rstep$ and the horizon sampled, but $\rstep<1$ on any non-trivial task makes collapse
at long horizon inevitable.

\subsection{The agentic task: deployed models collapse by $H{=}16$}\label{sec:agentic}
The streaming families could be dismissed as structured-output probes. The
\texttt{toolqa} family removes that objection: the agent runs a real ReAct loop,
issuing its own \texttt{inspect} tool calls to traverse a chain it cannot pre-plan
(Table~\ref{tab:toolqa}). Here degradation is starkest. Nearly every model solves
$H{=}2$ perfectly, but success collapses with horizon: GPT-4o-mini $1.00\to0.07$,
Claude-3-Haiku $1.00\to0.00$, DeepSeek-V3 $1.00\to0.27$, Qwen2.5-72B $1.00\to0.13$ from
$H{=}2$ to $H{=}16$. A self-directed tool loop is \emph{less} forgiving than the
streaming tasks: a single wrong hop is unrecoverable and the agent has no oracle to
catch it. That deployed, widely used models collapse within 16 agentic steps on a task
each solves perfectly at 2 steps is, we think, the paper's most striking result.

\subsection{Per-step reliability rises with scale---but never to 1}
Across the open ladder, per-step reliability rises with size (Pearson $0.36$ vs.\
$\log_{10}$ params), and the proprietary deployed models cluster with the strong open
models near $\rstep{=}0.68$--$0.72$ (Table~\ref{tab:rstep}). Scale and frontier
engineering buy per-step reliability with diminishing returns, but no model reaches
$\rstep{=}1$ on non-trivial tasks. Because success compounds as $\rstep^{\horizon}$,
every model is guaranteed to fall below any fixed reliability target at a sufficiently
long horizon; scale moves the cliff, it does not remove it.

\subsection{Degradation accelerates within a trajectory}\label{sec:accel}
A pure geometric law assumes a constant per-step hazard. The hazard instead
\emph{rises}: pooling long-horizon trajectories, mean per-step accuracy falls from
$0.58$ in the first third to $0.44$ in the last third (Figure~\ref{fig:perstep}). The
first error lands about a third of the way through; once an agent errs it rarely
recovers \citep{laban2025lostconv}. Acceleration makes the true decay
super-geometric, so geometric fits, if anything, understate long-horizon collapse.

\subsection{The driver is step count, not context length}\label{sec:cause}
Figure~\ref{fig:regime} contrasts the three regimes. The natural loop is best at every
horizon (pooled $0.75\to0.58$ from $H{=}2$ to $32$); both interventions are worse:
compressed ($0.64\to0.30$) and padded ($0.45\to0.23$). The decay slope per horizon
doubling is $-0.44$ (natural), $-0.69$ (compressed, $p{=}3\times10^{-6}$ vs.\ natural),
and $-0.40$ (padded, $p{=}0.51$, statistically indistinguishable from natural).
Bounding the context window \textbf{steepens} decay. This is decisive: if raw context
length drove degradation (lost-in-the-middle), bounding it would help; instead it
hurts---models rely on their accumulated reasoning history, and truncating it removes a
scaffold they depend on. Collapsing to a single prompt (padded) decays at the same rate
as natural from a lower baseline, so it is not the multi-turn structure either. What
remains is that degradation is intrinsic to executing many dependent steps. A direct
practical corollary: naive context truncation, a common production tactic,
\emph{backfires}.

\subsection{Mechanism}
The harness records how trajectories fail. Format and tool-call drift---a turn whose
output cannot be parsed as a valid action---reaches $21\%$ of trajectories and rises
with horizon, an interface-level failure mode. The first error concentrates around the
first third of the trajectory and per-step accuracy never recovers (\S\ref{sec:accel}):
errors are absorbing, and propagate silently---no oracle interrupts a plausible-but-
wrong intermediate state, much as hallucinated content survives without a verifier
\citep{huang2025hallucination}---compounding into trajectory-level failure.

\subsection{The benchmark gap, quantified}\label{sec:gap}
Using mean per-step reliability ($\rstep{=}0.61$) and the geometric law, we project task
success at representative benchmark horizons (Table~\ref{tab:gap}): from $0.42$ at
GAIA-length ($\sim$8 steps) to $0.30$ at SWE-bench-length ($\sim$30) to $0.24$ at a
100-step production horizon. (Means over our model$\times$task mix; the absolute values
depend on that mix, but the shape---a curve that keeps falling past benchmark
horizons---is the point.) Benchmarks sample the flat top of a curve that collapses out
of frame, and the agentic \texttt{toolqa} result (\S\ref{sec:agentic}) shows the
collapse arrives within just 16 self-directed steps.

\section{Discussion and recommendations}
\textbf{For practitioners:} estimate per-step reliability $\rstep$ on your own tasks and
treat the achievable horizon as $\log(\text{target})/\log \rstep$; because the hazard
accelerates, budget below the geometric estimate. Do not reach for naive context
truncation as a reliability fix---in our data it worsens decay. \textbf{For benchmark
designers:} report success \emph{resolved against horizon}, not a single aggregate, and
include horizons beyond the comfortable regime; a decay curve is far more predictive of
production behavior than a point estimate. \textbf{For the field:} per-step reliability
is the right primitive for agent evaluation---cheap to measure, multiplicative into task
success, and, unlike aggregate pass rates, extrapolable across horizons.

\section{Limitations and threats to validity}
Our tasks are synthetic, chosen for exact verifiability and a clean horizon knob; the
agentic \texttt{toolqa} family directly addresses the ``not really agents'' objection
with a real tool-use loop, but established benchmark environments (SWE-bench, $\tau$-
bench) remain future work. The benchmark projection (\S\ref{sec:gap}) is a placement on
the curve, not a literal per-benchmark score. We study nine models including frontier
deployed systems; the very largest proprietary models may have higher $\rstep$, but our
scaling analysis indicates this moves the cliff rather than removing it. Three small
models (Llama-3.2-3B, Gemma-3-4B, Phi-4-mini) were excluded because their hosted routes
did not adhere to the structured protocol---itself a mild instance of interface
fragility. We fix temperature at $0.2$; a temperature-sensitivity study is future work,
and the released harness supports it via one flag. The agentic $H{=}32$ cell was not
completed (API budget); we exclude it and report \texttt{toolqa} over $H\le16$, where
the collapse is already decisive. Hosted inference carries residual non-determinism
despite fixed seeds; we release raw outputs.

\section{Conclusion}
Agents rot geometrically. Task success follows $P\approx \rstep^{\horizon}$ in a per-step
reliability that rises with scale but saturates below $1$ on non-trivial tasks, so long
horizons guarantee collapse---and on a genuine agentic loop, even deployed frontier
models collapse within 16 steps. The decay accelerates within a trajectory, and its
driver is the number of dependent steps, not context length: bounding context makes
things worse. Because benchmarks sample short horizons, they report the flat top of this
curve and hide the collapse that defines production behavior. Measuring and reporting
per-step reliability against horizon would close much of the gap between what we
benchmark and what we deploy.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.} All code, task generators, prompts, seeds, raw
trajectories, and analysis scripts are released; every quantitative claim traces to the
released dataset.
